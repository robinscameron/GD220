This course focusses on the basics of digital photography. We will examine camera handling, technical considerations of exposure and depth of field, to mastering composition and working with any kind of lighting (natural + flash). We will also consider photographic history from varying eras beginning with the early 1900s all the way up to contemporary photographers. We will learn the basics of how to edit your photographs in Photoshop and Lightroom as well as honing our art director’s eye when choosing our best work. We will work with digital cameras to take many types of images, examining our work each week with subjects from place, to people and objects. We will also read about photography from photographers and critics and have a few guest speakers give us lectures on special topics.

Objectives

— Become familiar with key concepts of photography including: aperture + f-stop, lighting, editing, composition and camera techniques
—Increase confidence in classroom speaking and presenting
—Engage with photographic history and contemporary voices in photography
— Create a suite of compelling photographs that will be formatted into a portfolio
—Demonstrate understanding of how to take a candid photographs of people
—Demonstrate understanding of how to take landscape / architectural photographs in any kind of lighting
—Demonstrate understanding of how to photograph objects in both a documentary setting and also an art directed photoshoot
—Create a photo essay that explores your interests


Readings

Every second week there will be readings that will expand on the ideas discussed in the course. The readings are photography writings taken from many vantage points and from varying moments of art history. These readings will broaden your scope and allow you to be open minded about concepts that you may be able to utilize in your own work. We will have a brief discussion as a class each week about what we have read.

Presentations

Each week one student will be selected to give a brief presentation of a photographer that they have found that inspires them, the student will walk the class through details that they notice about the photographer in a detailed session. I’ll start us off with one so we know what to expect. There will be a sign-up sheet available here and inspiration links here.

Are.na sketchbook

In order to keep your ideas organized we will utilize the website Are.na. This will be a sort of ‘digital sketchbook’ for what you come across in your research. As part of your grade you will be expected to have an Are.na channel with at least 20 blocks of inspiration / images / digital content that you’ve been gathering for the course and your projects, there is a chrome extension that might come in handy. You can also take notes directly in Are.na if you like. At the end of the course there will be an option to print a PDF or a book of your findings

For each assignment you will be responsible for presenting at least six images. This small number of images does not mean less work, it means your process should be to take a many more images to practice composition, lighting and technique and edit to your best pictures. In your edit you are looking for the highest quality, the most well considered and compelling of the images you shot that day. You may reshoot and resubmit your images leading up to the final portfolio to improve your grade.

Tuesday February 2nd

— Assignment 1 Due: Place Shoot a landscape that has a distinct sense of place and some meaning for you. Consider what viewpoints work best and move around your place to get various angles. Experiment with different times of day i.e. the ‘golden hour’. Try out pattern, surface textures , haze and distance. See what kinds of winter light are available to you.

Tuesday March 9th

— Assignment 2 Due: Person Take candid pictures of a person that is in your immediate surroundings (COVID neutral risk) practice singling out the subject, overexposure vs underexposure. Experiment with different lighting situations, backlit, towards the light and use bounce cards or a reflective surface (anything white will work). Give your subject a sense of scale and find that critical moment in the arc of what they are doing.

Tuesday April 13th

— Assignment 3 Due: Object Set up a still life of objects that you have in close proximity to you, they could be a collection of the same or completely random (some ideas are, fruit, fabric, jewelry, flowers, vases, food). Try to pick objects that are label and brand neutral that you think would make a compelling picture. Think about sense of scale, artificial lighting, back lighting, format, harmony balance vs. asymmetry, high contrast even tone.

Tuesday April 27th

—Assignment 4 Due: Photographic Essay Create a visual essay focused on one subject and iterate on your chosen subject. For example: A visual essay on hands that uses both found re photographed images as well as the photographers hands

Tuesday May 11th

—Final Edited Portfolio Due as a PDF, utilize framing, pacing and scale to emphasize your work — 24 images minimum, 6 per assignment, impeccably edited and organized
